<p>How hot is your PC’s <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/416006/the-best-graphics-cards-for-pc-gaming.html">graphics card</a> temperature? During normal operations, your GPU temperature shouldn’t matter much—your graphics card should simply drive monitors and play games without overheating and shutting down. But if you have an older PC, a case with poor airflow, or if you tinker around with <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/3624581/how-to-benchmark-your-graphics-card.html">graphics card overclocking</a> or <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/604722/how-to-undervolt-your-graphics-card.html">undervolting</a>, being able to monitor your GPU temperature is vital—especially when the summer heat is scorching indoors and out. There’s a reason that “may your temperatures be low” is a common saying among PC enthusiasts. </p>
